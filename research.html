Research

My research interests are mainly in machine intelligence and evolutionary computation. Currently, I focus on machine learning, many-objective optimisation, and their applications in real-world systems.

Machine Learning

Machine learning is the science of getting computers to act without being explicitly programmed. Prof. Tom M. Mitchell provided a widely quoted definition of learning1.

Definition: A computer program is said to learn from experience EE with respect to some class of tasks TT and performance measure PP, if its performance at tasks in TT, as measured by PP, improves with experience EE.

For a learning system, the experince is the process of analysing the collected data (and providing predictions to interact with the enviorment). From this perspective, machine learning is learning from the data. Our work in machine learning includes manifold learning, sparse representation, kernel methods, and deep neural networks.

Many-objective Optimisation

Multi-objective optimisation, which addresses optimisation problems involving multiple (conflicting) objective functions to be optimized simultaneously, has attracted increasing interest in the evolutionary computation community. A multi-objective optimisation problem (MOP) can be generally formulated as follows: minxf(x)=(f1(x),f2(x),...,fm(x)),s.t.x∈Ω,gi(x)≤0,i=1,...,p,hj(x)=0,j=1,...,q,minxf(x)=(f1(x),f2(x),...,fm(x)),s.t.x∈Ω,gi(x)≤0,i=1,...,p,hj(x)=0,j=1,...,q,
where x=(x1,x2,...,xd)∈Ωx=(x1,x2,...,xd)∈Ω is the decision vector; f∈Rmf∈Rm is the objective vector. The decision vector xx is composed of dd decision variables and the objective vector ff is composed of mm objective functions, and objective function fi(x)fi(x) maps xx from ΩΩ to a scaler. gi(x)gi(x) and hj(x)hj(x) are known as the inequality and equality constraints, respectively. If an MOP has more than three objectives, it is called as an many-objective optimisation problem (MaOP). Our work in many-objective optimisation includes the design of many-objective evolutionary algorithms, test problem construction, and visualisation.

Blind Souce Separation

Blind source separation (BSS) aims at separating the original source signals from their mixtures without any a priori knowledge about the mixing matrix and the source signals. BSS has attracted considerable research attentions because of its wide applications in biomedical engineering, remote sensing, speech recognition, communication systems.

Generally, a linear instantaneous mixing system with nn input sources and mm outputs can be formulated as: x(t)=As(t)+e(t),x(t)=As(t)+e(t), where s(t)=[s1(t),s2(t),…,sn(t)]Ts(t)=[s1(t),s2(t),…,sn(t)]T is a nn-dimensional source vector, si(t)si(t) denotes the sample of the ii-th source at tt time instant, x(t)=[x1(t),x2(t),…,xm(t)]Tx(t)=[x1(t),x2(t),…,xm(t)]T denotes an mm-dimensional mixture vector, xi(t)xi(t) denotes the observed value of the ii-th sensor at tt time instant, e(t)e(t) is the additional noise, and A=[a1,a2,…,an]A=[a1,a2,…,an] is the mixing matrix, whose ii-th column is called the steering vector corresponding to the ii-th source.

A large number of BSS algorithms have been developed in the past decades, and most of them assume that the number of sensors is not less than the number of sources. In practice, however, this assumption is difficult to be satisfied. For example, in a wireless sensor network, the number of sources is sometimes unknown to the receivers. Thus, the number of the disposed receivers could be less than the number of the sources, which leads to a more challenging problem, i.e., underdetermined blind source separation (UBSS). It is a challenging and significant problem in signal processing community. Our reserach aims at achieving UBSS by exploiting the sparsity of sources.
